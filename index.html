Mar 2025 · Causal Inference

Why your A/B tests are probably wrong

A/B testing is the gold standard of experimentation — until it isn't. Most practitioners run tests correctly in a narrow technical sense but draw conclusions that don't hold up. Here's what usually goes wrong.

Peeking at results

The most common mistake: checking your experiment dashboard daily and stopping when you see a significant p-value. This inflates your false positive rate dramatically. Pre-register your sample size and stop date before running the test.

SUTVA violations

The stable unit treatment value assumption (SUTVA) requires that your treatment of one unit doesn't affect others. In any networked product — social, marketplace, collaboration tools — this assumption is almost always violated. Users in control see different behavior because treated users exist nearby.

When to reach for causal inference

When randomization is impossible or SUTVA is violated, consider difference-in-differences, regression discontinuity, or instrumental variables. These methods trade some statistical power for robustness to real-world complications.

The unreasonable effectiveness of linear models

There's a certain kind of data scientist who reaches for XGBoost before they've even looked at their data distribution. This post is a gentle corrective.

When linear models win

In many tabular regression tasks, a well-regularized linear model with thoughtful feature engineering matches or beats tree ensembles — especially when your training data is under 10,000 rows, when interpretability matters to stakeholders, or when your features have roughly linear relationships with your target.

The interpretability dividend

A coefficient is a story. You can walk into a meeting and explain exactly why your model gives the prediction it does. That matters enormously in regulated industries and in building trust with non-technical partners.

Oct 2024 · Research Practice

Writing reproducible research: a checklist

Reproducibility is the baseline expectation of science, yet it's routinely neglected in applied data work. Here's the checklist I actually follow.

Environment

Pin your dependencies. Use requirements.txt or renv. Docker if stakes are high. If someone can't recreate your environment in under 10 minutes, you haven't finished your analysis.

Data

Never modify raw data files. Use a pipeline that transforms raw → processed → features → model input with checksums at each stage. Document where the raw data came from and when you downloaded it.

Code

Set your random seeds explicitly. Use relative paths. Write functions, not scripts. Review your own notebook top-to-bottom before sharing — "Restart and run all" is not optional.
