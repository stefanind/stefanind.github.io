About

My main interests sit at the intersection of machine learning research, systems, and applied decision-making. On the research side, I am especially interested in knowledge distillation and representational learning: how models acquire useful internal structure, and how that structure can be transferred more efficiently to new models. Long term, I want to better understand how knowledge is formed inside neural networks and how training can be made more efficient through better objectives, teacher-student methods, and possibly better architectures.

I am equally drawn to applied ML work where the output matters. I enjoy building systems that take messy, real data and turn it into something reusable, interpretable, and useful for making decisions. My work has included building production data pipelines, designing reusable schemas and feature frameworks, extracting signal from unstructured text, and developing explainable models for decision-making.

My background is unconventional: before moving into data science, I studied philosophy at UBC. That training shaped how I reason under uncertainty, communicate ambiguous findings, and think carefully and critically about what a model is actually claiming. It is a big part of why I am drawn to work where rigor matters—whether that means improving training efficiency, building reliable ML systems, or producing analyses that inform decisions with real consequences.
